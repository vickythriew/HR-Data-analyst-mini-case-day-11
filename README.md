## Scenario

The HR Director requested an investigation after labor costs increased by **18%**, while production output increased by only **5%**.

My objective was to analyze workforce data and identify possible causes before making business recommendations.

## Key Performance Indicators

| KPI | Result |
|------|--------|
| Total Employees | 275 |
| Total Overtime Hours | 1,050 |
| Total Resigned Employees | 18 |
| Total New Hires | 13 |
| Average Attendance | 95.2% |

## Dashboard Findings

### Attendance

- Production has the lowest attendance (90%).
- Warehouse follows with 92%.
- Both operational departments require further investigation.

### Overtime

- Production records the highest overtime (520 hours).
- Warehouse records the second highest overtime (360 hours).

### Resignation Risk

| Department | Resignation Rate |
|------------|----------------:|
| Production | 8.33% |
| Warehouse | 7.50% |
| Finance | 4.00% |
| IT | 3.33% |
| HR | 0.00% |

### Overtime per Employee

Warehouse records the highest overtime workload per employee, indicating greater pressure on each individual employee despite having fewer employees than Production.

---

## Business Analysis

Instead of making immediate recommendations, I investigated relationships between attendance, overtime, resignations, hiring, and workforce capability.

Several possible factors may contribute to increasing labor costs:

- High overtime caused by workforce shortages.
- Loss of experienced employees.
- Knowledge transfer from senior employees to new hires.
- Temporary productivity reduction during onboarding.
- Workforce allocation between shifts.

Before making strategic decisions, I would also request:

- Production volume
- Machine downtime
- Shift schedules
- Employee leave records
- Training progress
- Recruitment status
- Workforce demographics

---

## Recommendations

### High Priority

Optimize shift allocation by distributing experienced employees across production teams before increasing headcount.

### Medium Priority

Monitor overtime trends and onboarding progress for newly hired employees to determine whether overtime decreases as experience improves.

### Long-Term

Review workforce planning and retention strategies to reduce employee turnover and improve operational stability.

---

## Executive Summary

Production and Warehouse show signs of workforce instability, where high overtime, lower attendance, and employee turnover are increasing labor costs faster than productivity.

The current data suggests operational inefficiencies rather than simply a shortage of employees. Additional operational data should be reviewed before making investment decisions such as hiring or salary adjustments.

---

## Key Learning

This case study taught me that dashboards should answer business questions rather than simply display numbers.

A good analyst reports data.

A great analyst explains **why the data matters** and recommends practical business actions supported by evidence.
